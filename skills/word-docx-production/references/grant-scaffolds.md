# NIH grant section scaffolds

These are **structural** templates (not sample text). Use them to scaffold content quickly while staying aligned with how NIH reviewers read.

Assumptions:
- You’re writing an NIH research application (e.g., F31/R21/R01) using a standard **Specific Aims** page and a **Research Strategy** with the canonical headers: **Significance, Innovation, Approach**.
- You will tailor lengths to the FOA. These scaffolds describe the *shape*.

---

## Specific Aims page

The Specific Aims page is a one-page sales document for the whole grant. Reviewers often form their initial “impact” impression here.

### Paragraph 1 — Problem + unmet need
- **Job:** State the high-level problem and why it matters *to NIH’s mission / patient/public health / biology*. Establish stakes.
- **Content:**
  - 1–2 sentences of “what’s broken” in the field/clinic.
  - 1 sentence on downstream consequences (burden, cost, mechanistic blind spot, failure of existing interventions).
  - 1 sentence on why *now* (new data/tool/shift makes it tractable).
- **Length:** ~3–5 sentences.
- **Reviewer sniff test:** Is this important enough to fund? Does the applicant understand the real problem?

### Paragraph 2 — Critical gap + what blocks progress
- **Job:** Define the **specific** knowledge/technology gap your proposal fills.
- **Content:**
  - “Despite X, we still do not know/cannot do Y.”
  - Name the bottleneck(s) explicitly (measurement, causal mechanism, model validity, scalability, reproducibility, generalizability, translation barrier).
  - If relevant: summarize *why* prior attempts failed (not enough resolution, wrong model, confounding, limited cohort, etc.).
- **Length:** ~3–6 sentences.
- **Reviewer sniff test:** Is the gap real, narrow, and researchable? Or is it vague (“more research is needed”)?

### Paragraph 3 — Long-term goal + objective + central hypothesis
- **Job:** Create a tight logic chain from goal → objective → hypothesis.
- **Content:**
  - **Long-term goal:** big picture direction (program-level).
  - **Overall objective:** what this project will do in this cycle.
  - **Central hypothesis:** a single defendable claim you will test.
  - **Rationale:** 1 sentence explaining why the hypothesis is plausible (preliminary data, prior work, strong theory).
- **Length:** ~4–7 sentences.
- **Reviewer sniff test:** Does the hypothesis actually drive the aims, or is it window dressing? Is it testable/falsifiable?

### Paragraph 4+ — Numbered aims
Each aim is a mini-unit: *question → approach → readout → expected outcome → interpretation → contingency (optional)*.

**Aim 1 (typically foundation / feasibility / key measurement)**
- **Job:** Establish the essential method/dataset/mechanism needed for downstream aims.
- **Aim text:**
  - 1 sentence: the aim’s scientific question.
  - 1 sentence: approach in plain language (avoid protocol dump).
  - 1 sentence: primary endpoint(s) / readout(s).
  - 1 sentence: expected outcome and how it supports/updates the hypothesis.
- **Length:** ~4–6 sentences.

**Aim 2 (mechanism / causal test / main discovery)**
- **Job:** The “meat” — the key test that drives novelty.
- **Aim text:** same structure as Aim 1.
- **Length:** ~4–6 sentences.

**Aim 3 (translation / generalization / model-to-human bridge)**
- **Job:** Demonstrate broader impact (different model, cohort, scale-up, robustness).
- **Aim text:** same structure.
- **Length:** ~4–6 sentences.

**Aim hygiene rules**
- Aims must be **independent but connected**. Avoid a domino chain where Aim 2 can’t happen if Aim 1 fails.
- Each aim needs **clear endpoints** a reviewer can assess.
- Don’t describe aims as “exploratory” unless the FOA tolerates it; instead, define the decision logic and outcomes.
- If an aim is high risk, include 1 sentence of **alternative strategy** (without bloating).

### Final paragraph — Innovation + outcomes + impact
- **Job:** Close the loop: why this is new, what you’ll deliver, and why it changes the field.
- **Content:**
  - 1–2 sentences on what’s innovative (conceptual + technical).
  - 1–2 sentences on expected outcomes/deliverables.
  - 1–2 sentences on impact if successful (health, biology, platform, policy, future trials).
- **Length:** ~3–6 sentences.

## Research Strategy

Write the Research Strategy to match how reviewers score: they want evidence of **importance**, **rigor/feasibility**, and **expertise/resources**. Organize with the canonical headers.

### Significance

**Paragraph 1 — Context and burden**
- Define the biological/clinical/public-health context.
- Tie to a real unmet need.

**Paragraph 2 — What’s known + what’s missing**
- Summarize current state *briefly*.
- State the critical gap(s) precisely.

**Paragraph 3 — How your project will fill the gap**
- Translate aims into “what changes if we win.”
- Make it obvious which gap each aim addresses.

**Paragraph 4 — Expected payoff**
- Downstream implications (mechanistic clarity, new targets, improved prediction, new platform, etc.).

Common mistakes:
- Long literature tours without a sharp gap.
- “Important because important” claims (no causal chain).
- Overpromising clinical impact without a translation path.

### Innovation

**Paragraph 1 — What is genuinely new**
- Novel concept/model, new method, new dataset, new integration.

**Paragraph 2 — Why the new thing matters**
- Explain what the innovation *enables* that current approaches can’t.

**Paragraph 3 — Risk/benefit framing**
- Preempt reviewer skepticism: acknowledge risk and show why reward justifies it.

Common mistakes:
- Calling “new to us” innovation.
- Listing tool names without explaining *the capability gain*.

### Approach

Anchor the Approach to the Aims. Treat each Aim as a mini-chapter with the same internal anatomy.

#### Per-Aim scaffold

**Aim header**
- Repeat the aim in 1 sentence.

**Rationale + working model**
- 1–2 paragraphs.
- Define the mechanism/model you believe.
- Cite preliminary data or strong prior evidence.

**Experimental plan**
- 2–6 paragraphs depending on complexity.
- For each experiment block:
  - **Design:** what you’ll do.
  - **Readouts:** primary/secondary endpoints.
  - **Analysis:** how you’ll decide (stats/thresholds/replicates).
  - **Interpretation:** what outcomes mean.

**Rigor + controls**
- 1 paragraph minimum per aim.
- Controls, blinding/randomization (when relevant), inclusion/exclusion, batch effects, normalization.
- For computational work: splits, leakage prevention, external validation.

**Potential problems + alternatives**
- 1 paragraph.
- Name the top 1–3 failure modes.
- Give alternatives that preserve the aim’s logic.

**Milestones / go–no-go**
- 3–6 bullets.
- Define decision points and what triggers pivot.

Common mistakes:
- Methods dump with no decision logic.
- No feasibility signals (no prelim data, no fallback).
- Aims dependent on fragile upstream results.

## NIH biosketch scaffold

The biosketch is part credentials, part argument: “This person can execute this project.” NIH is transitioning toward Common Forms + a Biosketch Supplement; requirements vary by FOA, but the rhetorical job stays consistent.

### Personal statement
- **Job:** Connect you to *this project*.
- **Structure:**
  1. 1 sentence: your domain identity.
  2. 2–3 sentences: the most relevant expertise (methods + topic).
  3. 1–2 sentences: specific evidence of performance (key papers, prior grants, leadership in a method).
  4. 1 sentence: how this uniquely positions you for your role.
- **Length:** ~6–10 sentences.

### Positions / education / training
- **Job:** Establish trajectory and legitimacy.
- Keep it factual and consistent with official records.

### Contributions to science
NIH traditionally allows up to 5 contributions. Treat each contribution as a “micro-abstract”.

**Per-contribution scaffold**
- **Contribution title:** 1 line.
- **Narrative (1 short paragraph):**
  - Problem context.
  - What you did.
  - What changed in the field.
- **Products:** list the key papers/software/datasets that prove it.

### Research support / current & pending
- **Job:** Demonstrate experience managing relevant work and avoid overcommitment concerns.
- List support in a way that makes effort plausible.

## Reviewer-oriented checks (quick)

- **Aims page:** can a reviewer summarize your project in 2 sentences after 60 seconds?
- **Approach:** does every major experiment have: readout → decision → contingency?
- **Risk:** are risks acknowledged *and* managed, or just denied?
- **Significance:** is there a clear “if we win, then…” chain?
