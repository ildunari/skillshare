# Narrative arcs

Use this guide to structure deck flow before choosing slide-by-slide archetypes.

## Why arcs matter

A strong deck is not a list of slides. It is a sequence of decisions:

- What the audience should believe
- Why they should believe it
- What they should do next

Narrative arcs convert raw content into that sequence.

## Arc selection matrix

| Arc | Best for | Core sequence |
|---|---|---|
| Problem -> Solution -> Proof -> Ask | Product and investor decks | tension, approach, evidence, decision |
| Current state -> Future state -> Plan | Transformation/strategy decks | baseline, vision, roadmap |
| Why now -> Why us -> Why this plan | Competitive or GTM narratives | urgency, differentiation, execution |
| Insight -> Implication -> Action | Data/evidence decks | finding, meaning, recommendation |
| Risk -> Mitigation -> Confidence | Compliance/operations decks | exposure, control, assurance |

## Section blueprint

Map the chosen arc into 3-6 sections:

1. Hook and context
2. Tension or problem
3. Evidence and mechanism
4. Decision frame
5. Execution plan
6. Close and next step

Not every deck needs all 6 sections, but every deck needs a visible progression.

## Transition quality checks

- Every section boundary should have a clear sentence-level handoff.
- Avoid jumping from problem to roadmap without evidence bridge.
- Use section-break slides (`A2`, `A22`, `A4`) every 4-6 dense slides.
- If two consecutive slides have identical rhetorical function, merge or diversify.

## Arc QA checklist

1. Can each slide be tagged with one rhetorical function?
2. Does the argument strengthen over time instead of repeating?
3. Is there at least one proof-heavy section?
4. Is the final ask or recommendation explicit?
