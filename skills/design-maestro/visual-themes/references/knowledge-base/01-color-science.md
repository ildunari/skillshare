## 1. Color science & visual perception

### 1.1 The screen color pipeline

Before choosing any color, internalize the path from number to perception:

1. **You choose values** in a color space (sRGB, Display P3, OKLCH).
2. The browser converts them through linear light and a profile connection space (XYZ).
3. The device maps them to physical primaries, white point, and gamma curve.
4. The viewer perceives them under context — surround adaptation, spatial frequency, simultaneous contrast, and fatigue.

Two consequences for theme work: "same math" does not mean "same perception" (linear RGB interpolation often looks non-linear), and color is relational — your UI is simultaneous contrast and figure/ground all the way down.

### 1.2 Why legacy color spaces fail for systematic palettes

**RGB** and **HSL** are defined by device primaries, not human vision. Equal numeric steps in HSL lightness do not correspond to equal perceived lightness: yellow at `hsl(60, 100%, 50%)` appears far brighter than blue at `hsl(240, 100%, 50%)`. The same chroma values can look muted for warm hues and neon-bright for cool ones. Palettes generated by rotating hue and adjusting saturation in HSL consistently produce inconsistent visual weight, unpredictable contrast, and "muddy" gradient midpoints caused by interpolation paths cutting through low-chroma regions of the space — often accompanied by the Abney effect (unexpected hue shifts, e.g., blue-to-white turning purple).

The 1976 CIE standards **CIELAB** and **CIELUV** were the first widely-used perceptual spaces, separating lightness (L*) from opponent color axes. They improved on RGB substantially but were optimized for reflective media and exhibit known non-uniformities, especially in blues. **HSLuv**, a cylindrical space grounded in CIELUV, offers consistent contrast across hues but has a smaller usable gamut than OKLCH — many saturated colors cannot be expressed.

### 1.3 OKLab and OKLCH: the current standard for palette construction

Björn Ottosson's **OKLab** (2020) and its cylindrical form **OKLCH** have become the practical standard for screen-based color work. OKLCH encodes three coordinates:

- **L (Lightness):** 0% (black) to 100% (white). Perceptually uniform — two colors at L=60% appear equally bright regardless of hue. This is the critical property HSL lacks.
- **C (Chroma):** Absolute colorfulness. Unlike HSL saturation (a 0–100% scale relative to gamut limits), chroma is an unbounded measure. In sRGB, chroma peaks around 0.37; Display P3 extends higher.
- **H (Hue):** 0–360°, perceptually uniform hue angle.

**What changes in practice:** OKLCH enables predictable lightness manipulation, wide-gamut support (P3 and beyond), smooth gradients without muddy midpoints, and human-readable parameters that map cleanly to design intent. The space does have gamut limits — some hues cannot reach high chroma at certain lightnesses (the cylindrical "crown" compresses near the neutral axis) — but these are predictable once understood.

**CSS implementation:** CSS Color Module Level 4 supports `oklch()` natively. Relative Color Syntax enables dynamic theming:

```css
/* Darken for hover while preserving hue and chroma */
--btn-hover: oklch(from var(--btn-color) calc(l - 0.1) c h);
```

### 1.4 Wide gamut: sRGB vs Display P3

Modern hardware has outgrown sRGB's narrow triangle. **Display P3** (Apple's adaptation of DCI-P3) covers approximately 86.9% of Pointer's gamut versus sRGB's 69.4%, enabling vivid oranges, greens, and pinks that sRGB cannot reproduce. The W3C Design Tokens specification explicitly supports Display P3 and OKLCH for cross-platform tokens.

**Theme strategy:** Treat P3 as an enhancement layer. Define the theme in perceptual terms (OKLCH) and render into sRGB or P3 depending on device capability. When a P3 color is displayed on an sRGB monitor, browsers perform gamut mapping — typically reducing chroma while preserving lightness and hue, which maintains the color's intent better than simple RGB clipping.

### 1.5 Google HCT: Material Design 3's approach

Material Design 3 uses **HCT (Hue, Chroma, Tone)** — a hybrid model with hue and chroma from CAM16 (a color appearance model) and tone (lightness) from CIELAB L*. This ensures colors generated dynamically from user content (wallpapers, photos) strictly adhere to WCAG contrast standards, since tone is directly correlated to luminance.

HCT is worth studying as an existence proof of constraint-based generation: a single source color produces five tonal palettes (primary, secondary, tertiary, neutral, error) across 10–12 tones, automatically assigned to semantic roles like `primary`, `primary-container`, `on-primary`.

### 1.6 What makes a palette feel systematic vs random

A palette feels intentional when it obeys constraints that are perceptually legible to humans — even if users can't name the constraints. Think of a systematic palette as:

- A small number of **hue anchors** (often 1–3)
- A controlled **chroma envelope** (saturation doesn't randomly spike)
- A consistent **lightness ladder** (steps that feel evenly spaced)
- A predictable mapping from **tokens to roles** (background / surface / text / border / accent)
- A limited set of **semantic exceptions** (error / warn / success / info), themselves internally consistent

This is why "pick 10 nice colors" always looks amateur: it's a bag of disconnected points, not a set of coordinated curves through perceptual space.

#### Building color ramps

**Step 1 — Define lightness range by role:**
- **Background:** Very high L, near-zero chroma (or very low L for dark UIs)
- **Surface:** Slightly separated from background (small ΔL)
- **Text:** Extreme L values (high on dark, low on light), low chroma
- **Accent:** Mid-to-high chroma, L chosen per state (default / hover / active)

**Step 2 — Interpolate in a perceptual space, not RGB/HSL.** Use OKLCH or OKLab. In CSS: `color-mix(in oklch, ...)`.

**Step 3 — Manage chroma across lightness so ramps stay natural.** Common failure: constant chroma while lowering lightness pushes colors out of gamut or into "radioactive" saturation. Apply a chroma bell curve — chroma peaks at mid-tones (L ≈ 50–60%) and tapers at extremes.

**Step 4 — Apply hue shifting for organic feel.** The Bezold-Brücke effect: higher luminance shifts hue perception. To compensate, shift warm hues toward yellow as they lighten, and cool hues toward cyan. A simple formula:

$$H_{adjusted} = H_{base} + k \times (1 - L_{normalized})$$

where $k$ is 5–10 degrees.

#### Gamut masking

Derived from James Gurney's techniques for traditional painting, gamut masking restricts available colors to a geometric shape on the color wheel (e.g., a triangle connecting cyan, orange, and neutral grey). Digitally: define a polygon on the OKLCH hue-chroma plane and clamp all generated colors to fall within it. This guarantees harmony by limiting the visual vocabulary of the theme — a more principled approach than just "picking complementary colors."

### 1.7 Color harmony beyond the classic wheel

The traditional complementary/triadic wheel is a teaching tool, not a system. It ignores perceptual non-uniformity, the role of neutrals, contextual effects, and semantic mapping. Modern approaches fall into three buckets:

**A) Constraint-based harmony (practical UI approach):**
Harmony emerges from consistency constraints rather than geometric wheel rules — shared temperature, shared chroma envelope, consistent tone ladder across accents, controlled hue spread (e.g., 20–40° band plus a small counter-accent). This is how most production design systems actually work.

**B) Distance/optimization approaches (research + tools):**
Treat harmony as an optimization problem in perceptual space: minimize "disharmony" / maximize preference with constraints on hue relationships, ΔE distances in OKLab, and palette balance. Research in palette-based harmonization (IEEE, 2025) uses geometric transformations of palette points to make sets coherent.

**C) Contextual / environment-aware models:**
Harmony depends on background, surround, and task (reading vs emphasis). In UI, harmony isn't just aesthetic — it's functional coherence. A "harmonious" palette that fails readability is not harmonious in practice.

A 2014 review of color harmony studies found that high lightness and moderate chroma generally correlate with higher harmony ratings, and that systematic variation in lightness or chroma across a palette increases perceived harmony. However, harmony is not purely geometric — cultural associations and context play significant roles, and cross-cultural differences were minor compared with individual preferences.

### 1.8 Color, emotion, and context

The relationship between color and emotion is real but complex. A 2025 systematic review covering **132 studies (42,266 participants across 64 countries)** found consistent patterns:

- **Light colors** tend to evoke positive emotions; **dark colors** evoke negative emotions.
- **Red** is associated with high-arousal emotions of both valences — excitement and love, but also anger. Its salience has biological roots.
- **Yellow/orange** elicits positive, high-arousal emotions (joy, energy).
- **Blue, green, and white** link to positive, low-arousal emotions (calmness, serenity).
- **Grey** conveys negative, low-arousal feelings (sadness, lethargy).
- **Black** associates with negative, high-arousal emotions (fear, grief).
- **Pink** evokes gentle, positive feelings.

Critically, emotional associations are driven by **interactions between hue, lightness, and saturation** — not hue alone. Brighter, more saturated versions of red and yellow intensify arousal, while desaturated or darker versions convey maturity or seriousness.

A separate 2025 study using text-to-image generative models found that high-chroma reds evoke joy, rage, and negative powerlessness simultaneously (strong emotions of both valences), and that lightness modulates feelings of serenity vs threat. This supports the idea that emotional response involves hue × chroma × lightness interactions, not simple categorical mappings.

**Cultural modulation:** Red symbolizes prosperity and celebration in many East Asian cultures but danger in Western contexts. White connotes purity in Western weddings but mourning in parts of East Asia. A 2024 industry report found that misinterpretation of color symbolism causes cognitive biases in approximately 62% of global brands — an inclusivity argument for semantic tokens that enable locale-specific color substitution.

**Practical application:** Use color-emotion associations as hypotheses, not rules. High-saturation palettes feel energetic but cause fatigue on large screens. Restrict high-chroma colors to accent roles; use low-chroma neutrals for surfaces. Validate with user testing, A/B experiments, and domain-appropriate heuristics (dashboards vs games vs finance vs medical).

### 1.9 Contrast and readability

#### WCAG 2.x: necessary but flawed

WCAG 2.x calculates contrast using relative luminance: $(L_1 + 0.05) / (L_2 + 0.05)$. This metric assumes luminance differences alone determine readability and imposes pass/fail thresholds (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text). The math breaks down at extremes: it rates dark-on-dark pairs as having lower contrast than perceived, and passes light-on-orange combinations that are actually illegible. It also ignores font weight, size, and polarity (dark-on-light vs light-on-dark).

#### APCA: perceptual contrast for readability

The **Advanced Perceptual Contrast Algorithm (APCA)**, part of the WCAG 3 draft, computes a **Lightness Contrast (Lc)** value that predicts perceived readability. Unlike WCAG 2.x ratios, equal Lc values correspond to equal perceived contrast, and the model incorporates spatial frequency (font weight and size).

**Key Lc thresholds:**

| Lc Value | Recommended use |
|----------|----------------|
| **Lc 90** | Preferred for body text (fluent reading) |
| **Lc 75** | Minimum for body text (~WCAG AAA equivalent) |
| **Lc 60** | Non-body text (short labels). Min font: 24px/400 or 16px/700 |
| **Lc 45** | Headlines / large text (~WCAG AA for large text) |
| **Lc 30** | Placeholder text or disabled elements |
| **Lc 15** | Non-text UI boundaries (dividers, input borders) |

**Critical property:** APCA is **polarity-dependent**. White text on black yields a different Lc than black text on white, reflecting the biological asymmetry in how human eyes process light vs dark stimuli. For dark mode, avoid pure white (#FFFFFF) on pure black (#000000) — use off-white (~#F5F5F5) on dark grey to reduce bloom and maintain comfortable Lc.

**Practical guidance for theme design today:**
- **Meet WCAG 2.2 AA** using contrast ratios (hard compliance requirement).
- Use **APCA as a second lens** when refining ramps, especially for thin fonts, small UI text, and low-contrast "premium" aesthetics where ratio-based checks are least accurate.

### 1.10 Gestalt principles: how perception drives coherence

Gestalt psychology provides the cognitive science behind "this looks good" or "this feels cluttered." Key principles for screen-based interfaces:

- **Proximity:** Closer elements feel related. This is the strongest grouping principle and white space is its instrument.
- **Similarity:** Shared color, shape, or size creates mental groups. Using the same accent color on unrelated elements causes unintentional grouping; differentiating colors enhances separation.
- **Common region:** Enclosing elements in a container (card, panel) overrides proximity. This is the fundamental principle behind card-based UI.
- **Connectedness:** Lines or visual links override proximity.
- **Continuity & alignment:** Misalignment reads as accidental and degrades perceived quality.
- **Figure-ground:** The brain separates foreground (interactive) from background (static). Drop shadows and scrims establish this relationship; in dark mode where depth perception is reduced, lighter surfaces indicate higher elevation.

**A 2025 study on hierarchical graphic information** found that structural cues (overlap, enclosure, grouping) dominate size cues in conveying hierarchy. When elements are separated, varying their size enhances perceived hierarchy — but the presence of physical grouping has a greater effect. This suggests designers should prioritize grouping and separation before adjusting font sizes or weights.

**Visual weight** — the perceived salience of an element — increases with contrast, size, saturation, thickness, and isolation. Saturated warm colors (red, orange) are perceptually "heavier" and advance toward the viewer; cool colors (blue, green) recede and feel lighter. There is no precise formula, but understanding these tendencies helps distribute attention across a layout.

**Negative space** is not empty — it defines relationships and creates cognitive breathing room. High-contrast or highly saturated palettes require larger negative space to avoid visual overload; minimal, low-contrast palettes can support denser arrangements without feeling busy. The ratio of negative space to content is perceptually associated with "premium" quality, while high density signals "utility."
