# Domain-specific constraints

Scope: use this file whenever the document belongs to a regulated, scholarly, legal, or highly standardized genre. Domain rules outrank generic Word best practices. Start here before making layout choices that could break compliance.

## Contents

1. General rule hierarchy

## 1) General rule hierarchy

Apply constraints in this order:

1. current sponsor, journal, court, institution, or regulator instructions
2. required templates and forms packages
3. house style or brand rules
4. this skill’s defaults

Never let generic polish break a domain rule. In regulated or submission-driven work, a plain compliant document beats a beautiful noncompliant one.

## 2) NIH and other U.S. research grants

### NIH / SF424 baseline

NIH application attachments are compliance-sensitive. Use the sponsor’s current instructions, not memory.

Core NIH formatting constraints for attachments:

- use 8.5 x 11 inch pages
- keep all margins at **0.5 inch minimum**
- use an **allowed font at 11 pt or larger**
- keep type density reasonable — NIH has historically required no more than **15 characters per inch** and **6 lines per vertical inch**, but verify against the current SF424 instructions
- smaller text inside figures is allowed only if it remains legible
- do not place ordinary running headers or footers in attachments unless the instructions explicitly require them

### NIH current-process notes

- Page limits vary by activity code, announcement, and form package. Check the current NIH page-limits table every time.
- The **Specific Aims** attachment remains a one-page persuasion document in standard NIH research applications.
- As of early 2026, NIH transitioned to Common Forms for the Biographical Sketch and Current and Pending support. Always verify the current instructions at grants.nih.gov before formatting biosketches — the format has changed from earlier versions.

### NIH rhetorical structure

Use the reviewer’s reading path.

**Specific Aims**
- open with the problem and unmet need
- identify the critical knowledge or capability gap
- state the long-term goal, current objective, and central hypothesis
- present numbered aims with clear readouts and outcomes
- close with innovation and expected impact

**Research Strategy**
- use the canonical section pattern when applicable: **Significance, Innovation, Approach**
- under each aim, make the logic explicit: rationale, experimental plan, endpoints, rigor, risks, alternatives, milestones

### NIH budget documents

- Use the **modular budget** format only when the mechanism and applicant type permit it and the request fits the modular threshold.
- Use the detailed **R&R budget** when the request exceeds the modular threshold or the mechanism requires detailed budgeting.
- Treat subcontract and subaward budget components as distinct required forms, not as narrative attachments.

### Budget justification structure

A budget justification narrative typically follows this pattern per cost category:

- **Personnel**: name, role, percent effort, and a brief justification of each person's contribution to the project
- **Equipment**: items over the institutional threshold, with cost and justification for each
- **Travel**: purpose, destination, number of trips, estimated cost
- **Supplies**: categories with estimated costs and rationale connecting supplies to the proposed work
- **Consortium/contractual**: scope of work summary, budget, and justification for each subaward
- **Other**: publication costs, participant support, specialized services, animal per diems

Keep justifications concise. Reviewers scan them for reasonableness and completeness, not for prose quality. Match the budget period structure to the sponsor's forms.

### Other grant sponsors

**NSF**
- Follow the current PAPPG and solicitation.
- Check the current PAPPG for approved fonts and minimum sizes. NSF has historically required specific typefaces at minimum 10–11 pt depending on the font family.
- NSF readers expect strict section ordering and page-limit discipline.

**Horizon Europe**
- Use the official template.
- The reference body font in the template family is Times New Roman or equivalent.
- Do not fill the page limit just because space exists. Horizon guidance explicitly treats concision as a virtue.


### FDA and regulatory submission packets

When a Word-origin document will be converted into a regulatory PDF, think beyond the page itself.

Good FDA-oriented defaults include:

- hierarchical bookmarks that mirror the table of contents
- predictable heading structure, ideally no deeper than needed
- live hyperlinks where cross-document navigation is expected
- visible blue hyperlink text paired with clickable areas that do not add distracting visible boxes
- stable section titles that survive PDF conversion cleanly

Do not treat regulatory packets like ordinary business reports. Navigation, bookmark hierarchy, and export fidelity matter as much as the page design.

## 3) Scientific manuscripts and journal submission

Journal instructions outrank everything else. Scientific manuscripts are one of the least forgiving categories for “generic nice formatting.”

### Safe manuscript defaults when no template overrides them

- serif body font, usually 12 pt
- double spacing for review copies when requested
- simple one-column layout
- page numbers, and line numbers if the journal requires them
- restrained styling and no branded report aesthetics

### APA 7 baseline

Use APA when the field, course, or journal requires it.

- APA defines **five heading levels**
- tables use a bold table number with an italic title above the table
- figures use a bold figure number with an italic title; captions or legends should match APA placement rules
- keep visual styling restrained and avoid vertical rules in statistical tables unless the specific output context needs them

### Publisher-specific examples

Use these as reminders to check the exact target guide, not as universal defaults.

**Nature**
- Nature article submissions are tightly constrained in word count and display items.
- Nature's Extended Data artwork guidance has historically used **RGB** rather than CMYK and capped resolution at **300 ppi**. Always verify the current submission guide at nature.com.
- Nature also gives one-column and two-column size guidance for tables and figure layouts. Size panels to the final format rather than treating Word as an infinite canvas.

**Science**
- Review copies are commonly prepared **double-spaced throughout**, including references and legends. Check the current submission guide for exact requirements.
- The submission guide specifies a narrow set of accepted fonts and U.S. letter formatting.

**PLOS ONE**
- manuscript text should be double-spaced
- do not use multi-column layout
- keep headings to **three levels maximum**
- include page numbers and continuous line numbers when required
- tables and figures should appear after first citation in the manuscript file for that workflow
- figures are cited in ascending numeric order
- figure files commonly need **300–600 dpi** depending on figure type — verify against current PLOS submission guidelines

**Cell-family journals**
- the manuscript file and the figure files may be handled separately
- figure titles and legends often belong at the end of the manuscript file rather than embedded under each figure in the review copy
- do not assume that a polished report-style figure placement matches submission requirements

**IEEE**
- use the official IEEE template whenever available
- cite figures and tables in numeric order
- figure captions begin with “Fig. 1.” in IEEE style
- image requirements commonly distinguish between **300 dpi photographs** and **600 dpi line art**

**ACM**
- review manuscripts are typically submitted in **single-column** format
- use the ACM template and its paragraph styles rather than inventing your own
- accessibility matters; alt text for figures is strongly encouraged in ACM workflows

## 4) Business reports and board materials

Business documents need fast navigation and controlled emphasis.

### Business report defaults

- title page or clear title block
- executive summary when the audience includes decision-makers
- TOC for longer reports
- short headings with a visible hierarchy
- tables and charts that foreground the key comparison
- appendices for detailed evidence

Use brand colors sparingly. The mark of a professional board paper is not “more branding.” It is compressed decision support: summary first, evidence second, appendix last.

### Board materials

- keep titles and section headings short
- front-load recommendations, risks, and asks
- use charts only if they beat prose
- write captions that state the takeaway, not just the subject
- use page numbers and clear section labeling because board packets are often printed or combined

## 5) Legal documents and contracts

Legal formatting is conservative because precision, citation stability, and referenceability matter more than graphic style.

### Court filings

Always check the specific court’s local rules plus any governing federal or appellate rules.

General reminders:

- many courts require **8.5 x 11 inch pages**
- **1 inch margins** are common
- body text is often **double-spaced**
- block quotations and footnotes are often **single-spaced**
- page numbering position can be prescribed
- local rules may dictate approved fonts and minimum sizes

Do not assume one jurisdiction’s rules transfer cleanly to another. A filing that is typographically elegant but locally noncompliant is unusable.

### Contracts and agreements

Use a stable contract spine:

- title
- date and parties
- recitals
- definitions
- operative provisions
- boilerplate
- signature blocks
- schedules, annexes, or exhibits

Rules for contracts:

- defined terms must stay capitalized and consistent
- numbering must be style-driven and stable
- cross-references must be checked after every structural edit
- signature blocks should be aligned and legible, not built from random tabs and spaces
- exhibits should be labeled consistently and cited exactly in the body

### Bluebook citation baseline

Bluebook (*The Bluebook: A Uniform System of Citation*) governs legal citation formatting in most U.S. contexts. Key rules for document formatting:

- Case names in italics in text, not underlined, when using Bluebook's practitioner format (Bluepages B1). Academic format uses the same convention in law review articles.
- Signal conventions (*see*, *cf.*, *but see*, *accord*) are italicized (Rule 1.2).
- Use short-form citations after the first full citation (*id.*, *supra*, abbreviated case names) per Rules 4.1–4.2.
- Pinpoint citations use specific page numbers after the reporter cite (e.g., *Smith v. Jones*, 500 U.S. 100, 105 (1990)).
- When in doubt, follow the jurisdiction's local citation manual first, Bluebook second. Many state courts and some federal circuits have their own citation conventions.

### Redlining conventions

- Use Word's built-in tracked changes, not manual strikethrough and color.
- Show insertions and deletions, not just the result — the counterparty needs to see exactly what changed.
- Keep formatting changes separate from substantive changes where possible. Reformatting noise in a redline obscures the legal changes.
- Provide both a clean copy and a redline for counterparty review.
- When a redline becomes unreadable due to volume, provide a summary of changes alongside the marked document.
- Preserve the audit trail: do not accept and re-edit tracked changes to hide the revision history. The redline is a professional deliverable.
- For multi-round negotiations, accept the prior round's changes before generating a new redline against the current clean version.

### Document comparison

Use Word's Compare Documents feature (or equivalent) to generate redlines between two clean versions rather than relying on accumulated tracked changes. This produces a cleaner, more reliable redline and avoids the corruption that builds up when tracked changes accumulate through many editing sessions. Combine Documents is useful when two people have independently edited copies of the same base document.

## 6) Technical documentation and scientific reports

For formal technical reports, the living U.S. reference is **ANSI/NISO Z39.18**. ISO 5966 is historically important but withdrawn, so use it as background rather than as a current controlling standard.

### Technical report pattern

A strong technical report often includes:

- title page
- abstract or executive summary
- table of contents
- list of figures and tables if numerous
- numbered sections
- references
- appendices
- revision history if controlled

### Procedure and safety hierarchy

For procedures:

- use numbered steps
- place warnings and cautions **before** the hazardous step, not after it
- use note, caution, and warning treatments consistently across the whole manual

The ANSI Z535.6 severity hierarchy, from most to least severe:

- **DANGER**: imminently hazardous situation that will result in death or serious injury if not avoided. Use a red background or border.
- **WARNING**: potentially hazardous situation that could result in death or serious injury. Use an orange background or border.
- **CAUTION**: potentially hazardous situation that could result in minor or moderate injury. Use a yellow background or border.
- **NOTICE**: important information not related to personal injury — property damage, equipment damage, or procedural compliance. Use a blue background or border.

Place the signal word in bold before the message. Match the organization’s visual treatment (icon, colored border or fill, bold signal word) and keep it identical across the entire document set. When the organization’s standard differs from ANSI Z535, follow the organization’s standard.

### Revision discipline

Technical readers often need to know what changed. Use revision tables, change bars, or revision summaries only when the document system expects them. Do not fake revision control with ad hoc colored text.

## 7) Theses and dissertations

Institutional manuals outrank all generic advice. Universities often prescribe margin sizes, page order, title-page language, approval pages, and heading behavior.

Common preliminary pages include:

- title page
- copyright page
- abstract
- acknowledgments
- table of contents
- list of figures
- list of tables

Common body and back-matter expectations:

- chapter numbering
- references or bibliography
- appendices
- Roman-numbered front matter and Arabic-numbered body

Treat university formatting guides like court rules: compliance first.

## 8) SOPs and controlled procedures

SOPs are operational documents, not essays. Make them easy to follow under real working conditions.

### Typical SOP structure

- title and document ID
- purpose
- scope
- responsibilities
- definitions if needed
- procedure
- records or forms generated
- references
- revision history
- approvals and effective date

### SOP formatting defaults

- one clear numbering system for procedure steps
- visible but restrained note, caution, and warning styles
- headers or footers with document control fields
- page numbering with total pages if required
- sufficient white space for use in print or on shared screens

Do not beautify an SOP into a brochure. Clarity, sequence, and change control are the priorities.
