# Writing & editorial standards

Scope: use this file when drafting, editing, proofreading, or standardizing the language inside a Word document. This file is about making the text easier to read, easier to trust, and easier to maintain.

## 1) Write for understanding, not for self-display

Plain-language guidance from U.S. federal and UK government sources converges on the same principles:

- write for the actual reader
- state the main point early
- organize information around the reader’s questions
- prefer active voice unless the actor is unknown or irrelevant
- use short sections, short paragraphs, and concrete words
- explain jargon when you cannot remove it
- use headings, lists, and tables to reduce search time

That is not “dumbing down.” It is reader respect.

### Practical defaults

- Put the conclusion or recommendation near the top in business and policy documents.
- Keep paragraphs to one main idea.
- Use the simplest accurate verb.
- Prefer “must” for requirements and “may” for permission; avoid vague “should” when the text is actually mandatory.
- Prefer “you” for instructions and user-facing procedures.
- Break long procedural prose into steps, not into longer prose.

### Sentence discipline

A readable document is not built from perfectly uniform short sentences. It is built from sentences whose length matches the complexity of the thought. Still, as a default:

- trim openings and throat-clearing
- remove redundant pairs such as “null and void” unless the legal genre requires them
- replace noun-heavy abstractions with verbs
- keep subject and verb close together
- put conditions before consequences only when the condition truly must come first

## 2) Choose one capitalization system and apply it consistently

### Sentence case

Default for business, technical, policy, and plain-language documents unless the house style says otherwise.

Examples:
- Executive summary
- Research strategy overview
- Risks and mitigations

Sentence case feels modern and quiet. It prevents overcapitalization and usually ages better.

### Title case

Use when the governing style requires it, especially in APA headings, many academic titles, and some legal or traditional report environments.

Examples:
- Executive Summary
- Research Strategy Overview
- Risks and Mitigations

Per CMOS 17th edition (§8.159), title case capitalizes the first and last words, all major words, and all words of four or more letters. Do not capitalize articles (a, an, the), coordinating conjunctions (and, but, or, nor, for, yet, so), or prepositions of three or fewer letters (in, of, to, for) unless they are the first or last word. "To" in infinitives is lowercase. This is the most widely used title-case system in English publishing.

APA 7 uses a similar rule but capitalizes words of four or more letters and both words in a hyphenated compound (e.g., "Self-Report Measures").

### Rules

- Do not mix sentence case and title case across headings at the same level.
- Do not capitalize every important-sounding noun in body text.
- For legal defined terms, capitalization has semantic force. Preserve it exactly.
- For figure and table captions, follow the document’s caption convention consistently.

## 3) Control acronyms and abbreviations

Acronyms save space only after they stop costing the reader effort.

Use these rules:

- define an acronym on first use unless it is universally obvious to the intended audience
- do not define an acronym you use only once
- if a document has many abbreviations, include an abbreviations list or glossary
- avoid starting many adjacent sentences with unexplained acronyms
- if the audience is mixed, lean toward fewer acronyms

Example:
“International Business Communication Standards (IBCS)” on first use, then “IBCS” later.

For grants and manuscripts, check sponsor or journal rules about abbreviations in abstracts, tables, and figure legends. Many journals require each table or figure legend to define abbreviations independently.

## 4) Choose the right note and citation system for the domain

### Footnotes versus endnotes

Use **footnotes** when the reader benefits from immediate access to citation or commentary. They are common in Chicago humanities style, legal writing, and some policy work.

Use **endnotes** when:

- the page is becoming visually crowded
- the publisher or house style requires endnotes
- notes are supplemental rather than essential to sentence-level reading

For most business and technical documents, heavy footnoting is a bad fit. Use references, source notes, or appendices instead.

### Citation-system overview

- **Chicago Manual of Style**: books, humanities, some policy and publishing contexts; notes-bibliography or author-date.
- **APA 7**: psychology, education, social sciences, many academic papers.
- **Vancouver / AMA**: medicine and health sciences, often numeric citation systems.
- **IEEE**: engineering and computing, numeric bracketed references.
- **ACM**: computing, usually through the conference or journal template.
- **Bluebook**: U.S. legal writing.
- **House style**: many businesses use source lines, end references, or simplified author-date systems instead of full academic citation style.

Do not improvise citations. Pick the system the domain expects.

## 5) Edit in passes, not all at once

Professional editing usually happens in layers.

### Structural pass

Check:

- does the opening orient the reader?
- do headings match the actual argument?
- is the order logical?
- should any section become a table, list, or appendix?
- is the summary too late?

### Line-edit pass

Check:

- concision
- repetition
- ambiguous pronouns
- jargon and undefined terms
- sentence length and rhythm
- weak verbs
- hedging or faux precision

### Fact and reference pass

Check:

- citations are present where required
- figures, tables, appendices, and sections are cited correctly
- numbers match across prose, tables, and charts
- defined terms stay consistent
- dates, names, and version labels align

### Final proof pass

Check:

- punctuation
- capitalization
- spacing
- list punctuation consistency
- cross-reference accuracy
- broken numbering
- leftover comments or review notes

A single “proofread” instruction is too vague. Know which pass you are actually performing.

## 6) Use standard editorial markup, but adapt it to digital review

Traditional proof marks still describe the actions editors take:

- **delete**
- **insert**
- **transpose**
- **stet**
- **close up**
- **new paragraph**
- **query**
- **lowercase / uppercase**
- **italics / roman**
- **spell out / abbreviate**

In Word review workflows, express these through tracked changes and comments rather than obscure margin marks. Use comments when rationale matters. Use tracked changes when the exact textual edit matters. Do not bury all feedback in one memo at the end of the document.

## 7) Use inclusive, bias-aware language deliberately

APA 7’s bias-free language guidance is a good general baseline even outside APA.

Principles:

- mention demographic traits only when they are relevant
- use precise, current, community-accepted terms
- avoid defining people by a condition or limitation when that is not the point
- prefer person-first or identity-first language according to the community or organization involved
- use singular “they” when gender is unknown or when the person uses it
- avoid unnecessary gendering of roles
- avoid stigmatizing language around health, disability, age, poverty, nationality, or race

Examples:

- Better: “participants with diabetes” or the community-preferred equivalent
- Better: “chair” instead of “chairman”
- Better: “people experiencing homelessness” when that phrasing suits the context and audience

In legal or quoted material, preserve the source faithfully but do not reproduce outdated language in your own narrative voice unless analysis requires it.

## Editorial defaults worth enforcing

- One space after periods.
- Straightforward date format consistent across the file.
- Numerals versus words chosen by the governing style guide, not by whim.
- Parallel headings at the same level.
- Captions that explain purpose, not just object.
- Requirements written as requirements, not softened into suggestions.
